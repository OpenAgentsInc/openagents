The **Autopilot Earn** model begins as something very simple: users run software on their computers, press **Go Online**, and earn sats by executing tasks. At the surface level this looks like a compute marketplace. Providers supply CPU/GPU cycles and perform jobs posted by buyers, and the network pays them via Lightning when work is completed. That is the visible product. But structurally, something deeper is happening. The economic structure you are building is not merely a compute market — it is a **generalized solver economy**. And once that structure exists, it naturally expands beyond compute into any task where agents can compete to satisfy an intent.

To understand why this matters, it helps to look at where the idea of a solver economy came from. In decentralized finance, particularly in Ethereum ecosystems, systems like **CoW Protocol**, **MEV searchers**, and various DEX aggregators use a model where users do not specify *how* a transaction should be executed. Instead they express an **intent** — for example, “swap this asset for that asset with these constraints.” A network of independent actors called **solvers** then competes to fulfill that intent. Each solver can choose different execution strategies, combine liquidity from multiple sources, or route across chains to achieve the best result. The solver that produces the best outcome wins the right to execute the trade and capture the associated fee or spread. This architecture has proven extremely powerful because it converts transaction execution into an open competitive market. Instead of a single system deciding how work is done, a decentralized set of actors constantly experiments with better strategies.

However, the existing solver economies are narrow. They exist primarily within blockchain trading infrastructure. MEV searchers compete over arbitrage and block ordering. CoW Protocol solvers compete over swap execution. These are lucrative markets, but they are limited to **financial transactions on blockchains**. The mechanism — intent broadcast followed by solver competition — is powerful, but its application domain has remained constrained.

The **Autopilot Earn model** changes that. By combining an open job protocol (NIP-90), Lightning-native payments, and agent software running on millions of machines, you effectively create a system where **any type of task can be expressed as an intent and fulfilled competitively by providers**. Compute jobs are simply the first example. When a buyer posts a NIP-90 job request, they are effectively publishing an intent: “produce this output given these parameters, and I will pay this amount.” Multiple providers can see that request, decide whether to execute it, and compete on speed or cost. The provider who successfully delivers the result receives payment. This is structurally identical to a solver market — the only difference is that the task is computation rather than financial routing.

Once you recognize this equivalence, the implications expand quickly. If agents can compete to solve compute jobs, they can also compete to solve **liquidity routing problems**, **data retrieval tasks**, **API orchestration**, **model inference pipelines**, or any other structured job that can be expressed as an intent. The economic primitive becomes universal: publish an intent, let a market of autonomous agents compete to fulfill it, and reward the successful solver. The job might be generating text, retrieving data, converting currencies, routing payments, running a simulation, or performing arbitrage. The network itself does not need to know how the solver achieved the outcome; it only needs to verify that the result satisfies the contract.

This is where the connection to **Hydra and liquidity solvers** becomes particularly interesting. In the Hydra architecture, liquidity routing can be expressed as an intent just like a compute task. For example: “convert this amount of BTC to another asset within these constraints.” Instead of writing a single internal algorithm to perform the swap, Hydra can expose that routing problem to a network of solvers. Each solver may have different liquidity sources, capital availability, or routing strategies. The solver that produces the best outcome executes the route and earns a fee. From the perspective of the system, this is the same pattern already used in Autopilot Earn: a task appears, providers compete to complete it, and the winner receives sats.

At that point the boundaries between **compute providers and liquidity solvers begin to blur**. Both are simply providers in the OpenAgents network. One type supplies CPU or GPU cycles. Another type supplies capital and routing capability. Both are running software agents that monitor the network for opportunities to fulfill intents. Both earn revenue when they successfully complete work. Both build reputation based on performance metrics derived from receipts. And both contribute to the overall economic activity of the network. The only difference is the nature of the work they perform.

This convergence is what makes the model powerful. Historically, compute marketplaces and financial routing markets have been separate systems. Cloud compute platforms allocate tasks to machines, while DeFi protocols route liquidity between assets. In an agent-based network, these functions can coexist within the same economic fabric. A user’s Autopilot might execute AI inference jobs in one moment and fulfill liquidity routing intents in the next. The same machine and software stack participates in multiple markets simultaneously. Capital and compute become interchangeable inputs to a broader system of agentic work.

The result is a **generalized solver economy** — one that extends far beyond blockchain trading. Instead of a narrow class of arbitrage bots competing over transaction ordering, you have millions of autonomous agents competing to satisfy a wide variety of intents across the internet. Every time an intent appears — whether it is a compute request, a payment routing problem, or a data transformation task — the network becomes a marketplace of strategies attempting to solve it. Over time the best strategies win more work, earn more revenue, and reinvest that capital into better infrastructure or models. The system evolves through competition.

Another important implication is that this model aligns naturally with **Bitcoin-based payments**. In many existing solver economies, payments are mediated through complex token systems or captured through block-level incentives. In the OpenAgents design, the reward for solving work is simply **Lightning payments**. When a solver completes a task or successfully routes liquidity, they receive sats directly. This creates a clean feedback loop between work performed and value received. The currency used to reward solvers is the same one used to fund tasks, store capital, and measure profits. The economy becomes simpler and more legible.

Over time, the network begins to resemble a **distributed operating system for economic work**. Intents are analogous to system calls. Solvers are analogous to processes competing for execution. Hydra provides the liquidity substrate that ensures payments can flow reliably between participants. And Autopilot acts as the local agent that connects individual machines to the global marketplace. The system coordinates millions of independent actors through shared protocols rather than centralized scheduling.

From a strategic perspective, this means Autopilot Earn is not merely an onboarding feature for compute providers. It is the **entry point into a much larger economic architecture**. The first loop — running a hash job and earning a few sats — proves that the network works. But the underlying mechanism is capable of coordinating far more complex markets. Once the solver pattern spreads across multiple domains of work, the OpenAgents network becomes a universal platform where agents compete to solve problems and earn value.

In other words, the system you are building does not simply replicate existing compute marketplaces or DeFi routers. It unifies them. The same network that pays agents to run inference tasks can also pay them to route liquidity, process data pipelines, or orchestrate services across the internet. By structuring work as intents and rewarding successful solvers with Lightning payments, OpenAgents effectively generalizes the solver economy to **all forms of agent-executable work**. That is why the implication is so significant: the architecture you are launching as a simple “earn sats” feature has the potential to become the foundational economic layer for autonomous software agents.
