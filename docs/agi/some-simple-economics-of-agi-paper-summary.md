The paper **“Some Simple Economics of AGI”** argues that the central economic bottleneck in an AGI-era economy will not be intelligence or execution, but **human verification bandwidth**: the scarce ability to check, validate, audit, insure, and take responsibility for AI-generated outcomes. 

The authors’ core idea is that AI drives the **cost to automate** downward, while the **cost to verify** remains constrained by human time, expertise, and real-world feedback delays. This creates a widening **Measurability Gap**: AI can increasingly execute tasks that humans cannot affordably or reliably verify.

The paper reframes automation around **measurability**, not skill. In this view, AI does not only automate routine work; it automates anything that can be measured well enough. That includes many tasks once thought creative, analytical, or expert-driven. As measurable execution becomes cheap, economic value migrates to what remains scarce: trusted verification, ground truth, provenance, liability, status, and human meaning.

The paper’s major mechanisms are:

1. **Measurability-biased technical change**
   Jobs are disrupted based on whether their outputs can be measured and verified, not whether they are “high skill.” Highly educated work can be commoditized if its feedback loops are measurable.

2. **The Measurability Gap**
   AI’s ability to act expands faster than humans’ ability to verify. This creates a zone where deployment is cheap but oversight is too costly or too slow.

3. **The Missing Junior Loop**
   If AI automates entry-level work, people lose the apprenticeship pathway that builds future experts. This weakens the future supply of human verifiers precisely when verification becomes more valuable.

4. **The Codifier’s Curse**
   Experts who verify AI outputs create the training data and ground truth that help automate their own expertise.

5. **Alignment drift**
   Alignment is treated not as a one-time fix, but as an ongoing maintenance problem. If verification cannot keep up, systems drift toward optimizing measurable proxies rather than human intent.

6. **The Trojan Horse externality**
   Unverified AI output can look productive in metrics while hiding long-term risk. The authors call this “counterfeit utility”: measured activity rises, but real human value and institutional trust may decay.

The paper contrasts two possible futures:

**The Hollow Economy** is the bad equilibrium: AI generates enormous nominal output, but much of it is unverified, misaligned, or low-trust. Human expertise decays, AI verifies AI with correlated blind spots, and hidden risks accumulate.

**The Augmented Economy** is the good equilibrium: society scales verification alongside AI execution using observability tools, cryptographic provenance, synthetic practice, human augmentation, liability regimes, and better ground-truth infrastructure.

The paper gives different strategic implications:

For **individuals**, the advice is to move away from commodity execution and toward intent-setting, verification, liability underwriting, and meaning-making. People should use AI for synthetic practice, fast experimentation, and building verifiable track records.

For **companies**, the main moat is no longer raw output, but trusted outcomes. Firms should build verification infrastructure, collect verification-grade data, organize around a “sandwich” model—human intent → AI execution → human verification—and price products around liability and risk.

For **investors**, the attractive opportunities are not generic AI wrappers, but verification tools, provenance systems, synthetic practice platforms, human augmentation, liability-as-a-service, agentic infrastructure, and businesses with defensible ground truth.

For **policymakers**, the key market failure is that firms can capture the upside of unverified AI deployment while socializing systemic risk. The authors recommend liability regimes, mandatory auditability, public ground-truth infrastructure, investment in human augmentation, and international coordination around verifiable safety.

The figures reinforce the argument: page 44 maps tasks into zones based on whether they are cheap to automate and cheap to verify, highlighting the dangerous **Runaway Risk Zone** where AI can act cheaply but humans cannot verify affordably. Page 48 shows how automation can erode human experience unless synthetic practice compensates. Page 49 shows alignment declining as the measurability gap widens unless oversight capacity rises. Page 78 summarizes individual career strategy: displaced workers, directors, liability underwriters, and meaning makers.

In plain terms: **the paper says AGI makes execution abundant, but trust scarce. The winners—people, firms, investors, and governments—will be those who can verify, insure, and give meaning to AI-generated outcomes rather than merely produce more of them.**
