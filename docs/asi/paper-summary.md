The paper **“From AGI to ASI”** by Google DeepMind authors examines what might happen *after* human-level artificial general intelligence is reached, focusing on possible routes from AGI to artificial superintelligence and what might slow or stop that transition. 

The authors define **AGI** as roughly median human-level performance across most cognitive tasks, while **ASI** means a system or collective that exceeds large, well-coordinated groups of human experts across virtually all domains of human activity.

Their central claim is that, if AGI is achieved, it is unlikely that AI progress would naturally stop exactly at human level. Progress might plateau before AGI, continue gradually beyond AGI, or accelerate rapidly toward ASI—especially if recursive AI-assisted AI research becomes powerful.

The report identifies **four possible pathways from AGI to ASI**:

1. **Scaling compute, models, and data**
   Continued growth in compute, model size, data, and algorithmic efficiency could keep improving AI capabilities. Even if individual models plateau, running many AGI instances could produce collectively superhuman performance.

2. **Algorithmic paradigm shifts**
   New architectures, learning methods, memory systems, world models, continual learning, or more efficient algorithms could overcome limits of today’s transformer-based foundation model paradigm.

3. **Recursive self-improvement**
   AI systems could accelerate AI R&D by designing better algorithms, hardware, training data, experiments, or research processes. If this feedback loop becomes strong, it could cause very fast capability growth.

4. **Multi-agent coordination and group agency**
   ASI might emerge not from one “genius” model, but from large networks of AGI agents organized like companies, markets, research institutions, or coordinated collectives.

The paper also lists major **bottlenecks and frictions**:

* **Data wall:** human-generated high-quality data may run out, though synthetic data, simulations, self-play, and interaction data could help.
* **Economic and natural resource constraints:** compute, chips, energy, datacenters, supply chains, and investment may not scale fast enough.
* **Limits of the current neural paradigm:** today’s pretraining-plus-posttraining approach may hit diminishing returns.
* **Research getting harder:** future AI progress may require much more effort, though AI researchers could offset this.
* **Abstraction barrier:** models trained mainly on human abstractions may struggle to invent fundamentally new concepts from raw reality.
* **Deliberate slowdown:** regulation, accidents, misuse, social backlash, or governance could slow capability growth.

A key theme is uncertainty. The authors argue that no one can reliably forecast whether progress from AGI to ASI would be slow, smooth, or explosive. They call for a large interdisciplinary research agenda focused on forecasting, benchmarking beyond human-level performance, recursive improvement dynamics, multi-agent scaling laws, theoretical limits of superintelligence, safety, alignment, and societal impacts.

In plain terms: **the paper is a map of how AI might move from human-level intelligence to systems vastly beyond human collectives, and a call to study the technical, economic, physical, governance, and safety constraints before that transition happens.**
