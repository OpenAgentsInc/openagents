## 1. What the episode says

Episode 234 opens with a confession: "not everything we've talked about in the
223 episodes... has fully made it to production in a reliable way." Instead of
burying that, the episode turns it into the product: take "what's actually
live" and make it "a concept that agents can verify and tell you." The
deliverable is two-sided — a human page at `openagents.com/promises` and an
agent-friendly version agents "can pull... programmatically to see exactly
what's live and what's not."

The methodology claim is the interesting half. The speaker "pointed agents at
all of the most recent transcripts" — back to Episode 200 — with the
instruction: everything talked about in these episodes must have an associated
product promise, audited for "to what extent it is live or not." Gaps become
targets for coding agents, "and we may turn some of these actually into
bounties." The on-screen registry shows the first harvest: 25 promises,
state-mapped (5 green, 18 yellow, reds and withdrawals), with IDs like
`promises_registry_v1` (green), `pylon_v0.3_release_candidate_v1` (yellow),
`autopilot_historical_claude_code_me` (withdrawn), and a long red tail of
revenue-stream and marketplace claims. Some reds are attributed simply to "we
just have not pulled them into the new monorepo." The framing promise: "you'll
see these things start to flip more into green," and users can point their
agents at the page anytime and ask "what's live? What can we do?" The episode
is announced as "episode two of five we are launching today."

## 2. Where it stands against Revision 31

This is the arc's cleanest came-true story, and it came true three times over.

First, the registry itself became load-bearing infrastructure. `docs/promises/`
owns the records. The public API lives at `/api/public/product-promises` with
`/transitions`, `/audit`, and readiness descendants. `/promises` is one of only
three retained web product routes (alongside `/` and `/forum`). The
promise-integrity chain — stable docs/report paths, registry/transition/audit
APIs, transition authority, dereferenceable receipt/verification/evidence
refs — is an explicit preservation invariant: a green claim may not lose its
evidence. The episode's throwaway "agent-friendly version" is now the surface
agents are onboarded to at `openagents.com/AGENTS.md`.

Second, the idea scaled down. The 2026-07-03 owner mandate turned every stated
UX expectation into a behavior contract — statement verbatim, source recorded,
oracle test in the normal sweep — with the shared schema and coverage checker
in `packages/behavior-contracts`. That is the promise pattern applied at the
grain of a single sentence the owner said in a session.

Third, it scaled into issue workflow: issue-level UX promise contracts such as
#8675's `openagents_desktop.seam.codex_loss_accounted_history.v2` bind an
individual seam to a named, versioned contract ID. One episode's transparency
gimmick became the repo's three-tier claim discipline: public promise registry,
behavior-contract registry, per-issue seam contracts.

The intake side also hardened along the episode's lines: report intake is
Forum-first (the Product Promises Forum), and GitHub issues are restricted to
the strict-bug form. What did not carry: the bounty aside. Promises never
became paid bounties — money-movement is not P0, own-capacity closeouts are
no-spend, and the bounty idea has no owner-flip defined. And many of the
on-screen promise IDs name surfaces since retired (Autopilot pages, Sites,
Pylon revenue streams). Their reds and withdrawals are the registry doing its
job, not a defect in it.

Proof-rung read: the registry, APIs, and integrity chain are live-proven and
retained-route protected. Behavior contracts are enforced in the test sweep.
bounties sit below code-landed — direction with no promise.

## 3. What to extract and carry forward

- **Confession as product.** The durable move is converting "we overclaimed"
  into a machine-verifiable ledger instead of quieter copy. That is now the
  claim protocol writ large: every public statement either maps to a registry
  state with evidence or does not get made.
- **Transcript-to-promise auditing is a repeatable loop.** Pointing agents at
  the episode corpus and demanding a promise record per claim is exactly the
  lane this analysis file belongs to. Keep the loop alive: new episodes and
  owner statements should keep landing as promises or behavior contracts in
  the same change, never conversation-only.
- **Withdrawal is a first-class state.** The registry's willingness to show
  `withdrawn` and red tiles publicly is what makes green mean anything. Sarah's
  removal, Khala client deprecation, and the Cloudflare exit all had clean
  places to land because withdrawal was designed in from Episode 234.
- **The three-tier grain.** Public promises for product claims, behavior
  contracts for stated UX expectations, seam contracts for issue-level
  invariants. Pick the right tier. Never leave a stated expectation untiered.
- **Agents as the primary reader.** The episode's "your agent can be like,
  'okay, but what's actually live?'" is the reason the API shape matters more
  than the page. Keep the machine-readable surface first-class as apps churn.

## 4. How to make it come true

The registry is true. The residue is coverage for what Revision 31 is actually
building. The current promise set was harvested from the Episodes 200–234 era
and skews toward retired surfaces. The new product shape — OpenAgents web,
the greenfield OpenAgents mobile app, and OpenAgents Desktop — needs its own
promise records: the planned `openagents.mobile_app.v1` and
`openagents.desktop_app.v1` promises should be authored as the apps climb the
rungs, entering the registry no greener than their actual proof state
(code-landed or fixture-proven at first, green only at live-proven with
evidence refs).

Historical IDs need tending, not deletion. `khala_code.*` and other promise
IDs bound to deprecated clients must stay dereferenceable as withdrawn or
historical records, because the integrity chain forbids a claim's evidence
trail from going 404 — the registry's memory is the feature.

Bounded next leaves, without inventing issue numbers: (1) a coverage sweep
that diffs Revision 31's stated product shape against the registry and files
missing promise records, repeating the Episode 234 audit against the current
roadmap instead of old transcripts. (2) A freshness discipline for green
promises whose underlying surfaces churned in the Cloudflare-to-GCP move, so
each green's evidence refs still dereference on the current stack. (3) The
bounty idea stays parked behind a post-R7 revisit gate — pricing promise gaps
is settlement-adjacent work and out of scope while commercial expansion is
frozen.

Acceptance in six-rung vocabulary: new app promises enter at code-landed and
must climb fixture-proven → deployed → live-proven before green. Historical
promise dereferenceability is testable now and belongs in the sweep.
promise-backed bounties remain direction until an owner-armed settlement
decision exists.

## 5. Risks and tensions

- **A green claim may not lose its evidence.** Platform churn is the active
  threat: the GCP migration, route retirements, and app rewrites can silently
  break evidence refs under promises that stay green on the page. Freshness
  checking is part of the invariant, not an optional nicety.
- **Coverage debt inverts the value.** A registry that describes the 2026-06
  product while the repo builds the 2026-07 product misleads exactly the
  agents it was built to inform. The mobile/desktop promise records are the
  test of whether the loop still runs.
- **Scope inflation dilutes signal.** The episode's own instinct — a promise
  for everything ever mentioned — produces long yellow tails that read as
  noise. Behavior contracts absorbed the fine grain. Keep the public registry
  at product-claim altitude.
- **Bounty talk without settlement is a promise violation in waiting.** "We
  may turn some of these into bounties" must not reach public copy while
  closeouts are no-spend and `payoutClaimAllowed` is false. Copy stays inside
  the registry.
- **The registry is not roadmap authority.** Agents reading `/promises` learn
  what is live, not what is next. Sequencing lives in Revision 31. Keep the
  two authorities from blurring in onboarding copy.
