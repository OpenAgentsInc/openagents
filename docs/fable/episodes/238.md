## 1. What the episode says

A whiteboard episode announcing the live Tassadar distributed training run
under the banner "SHARE COMPUTE, EARN BITCOIN": "The Tassadar run is live...
it is paying people for contributing compute, and we are ready to start getting
more people on it."

The board claims two "World Firsts," a term the host borrows from MMO raiding
guilds ("they always shout out whenever they take down a dragon for the first
time in the world"): the first AI model training run paid in Bitcoin —
"specifically, to consumer compute. Anybody can plug in a computer and get paid
Bitcoin" — and the first public LLM-computer training run.

A "WTF is 'LLM-computer'?" section grounds the research: the idea comes from
the lab Percepta, and the model "executes computation exactly. Programs — like
CPU computation programs — are compiled into transformer weights. There is no
gradient descent," with gradient descent as an optional later add-on for
hybrids with conventionally trained models.

The "Live Money Loop" is the operational heart. The front door is
`openagents.com/agents.md` — "that will always be the front door for agents."
An agent joins the run, downloads Pylon ("our software that actually runs
this"), and enters the loop: CLAIM WORK → WORKER RUNS JOB → VALIDATOR REPLAYS
→ VERIFIED → PAY W + V. On payment the board writes "PAY 5K TO W + V" while
the narration says "five Bitcoin sats each," with an honest price caveat
either way: "We may change that or change the time windows... Prices could
change."

"Learning by Construction" sketches the module economy: verified program →
module → library grows → compose modules, with "more capability over time" —
"like an agentic npm. There is no npm for agents, right? Everyone's building
their own thing." The host adds that this registry could "preemptively solve
for some of the security and trust constraints by attaching better
cryptographic verification or payments," teasing a plugin-marketplace reboot.

"The Flywheel" closes the loop: VERIFIED WORK → BETTER MODEL → LOWER COST →
MORE DEMAND. The host argues no big lab is doing this because "the big labs
are happy for you to pay 10,000 times — or 10,000 different developers pay
10,000 times for the same function — whereas really that should be represented
in a module that is discovered in a registry... they are solving for their IPO
economics. We want to solve for you." The sign-off invites adversarial
review: "We are not going to be making any big claims that are not sourced by
evidence. We've got a pretty serious evidence pack built into all this...
Point your agents at our forum."

## 2. Where it stands against Revision 31

- **The headline product is retired.** Tassadar distributed training and the
  Psionic lanes were retired from the active openagents plan in the
  2026-07-08 docs-direction cleanup. The machine-work economy the run
  embodied remains long-horizon vision, not P0, which is strictly reliable
  Desktop/mobile coding, fleet software, and remote-first portable sessions.
  The LLM-computer research survives as the essays in `docs/tassadar/`.
- **The loop's verification step became the whole architecture.** WORKER RUNS
  JOB → VALIDATOR REPLAYS → VERIFIED is the direct ancestor of today's
  "verification is the load-bearing wall" regime: the six proof rungs
  (code-landed → fixture-proven → deployed → live-proven → owner-accepted →
  closed), accepted/rejected/failed/unknown_pending_reconcile closeouts,
  body-free command receipts, and the Work Unit / Execution Receipt /
  Delivery Receipt ladder — "'Agent completed' is never a proxy for 'work
  landed.'" The episode drew that wall on a whiteboard. The current repo
  enforces it in schemas and tests.
- **Pylon survived by changing jobs.** The "software that actually runs this"
  outlived the run it was built for: `apps/pylon` is now the contributor/
  local execution runtime powering typed Khala coding delegation — claim,
  execute, prove, close out — not a training-node product. The loop's shape
  persists. The payload changed from training windows to coding assignments.
- **The front door survived verbatim.** `openagents.com/AGENTS.md` is still
  the live agent onboarding surface, exactly as promised: "that will always
  be the front door for agents" is one of the episode's few present-tense
  true claims.
- **The payment leg exists but is decoupled from P0.** Bitcoin rails are real
  (BOLT12 tipping with settled receipts, the treasury wallet, the payout
  bridge), but current own-capacity work closes out `paymentMode: no-spend`,
  `settlementState: not_applicable`, `payoutClaimAllowed: false`.
  Marketplace/pooled-capacity resale is explicitly out of scope, and
  owner-local subscription capacity is never resold.
- **The evidence-pack sign-off got institutionalized.** "No big claims that
  are not sourced by evidence" is now the claim protocol, the challenge ledger
  with falsifiers, behavior contracts with oracles, and the promise-registry
  rule that a green claim may not lose its evidence.
- **The agentic npm and the flywheel are post-R7 direction.** No module
  registry, no inference-cost flywheel, and no marketplace exist in the
  current plan. Commercial expansion is frozen during R0–R7.

## 3. What to extract and carry forward

- **Replay as the trust primitive.** The episode's deepest idea is that
  verification means independent re-execution, not attestation. That lineage
  runs unbroken into the current receipt discipline and should be cited as
  the origin story: exact execution makes replay decidable, and replay makes
  payment (or acceptance) safe. Any future labor market re-runs this exact
  loop with today's typed receipts as the wire format.
- **Pay the verifier, not just the worker.** "PAY W + V" priced the
  validator's work equally with the worker's. Current closeouts still
  underprice verification culturally (it reads as overhead). The episode's
  symmetry — verification is paid work — is worth preserving when payouts
  return.
- **One canonical agent front door.** `agents.md` as the permanent,
  machine-readable entry point is a kept promise. Every onboarding surface
  (khala fleet connect, the Forum, the promise API) should keep hanging off
  it rather than multiplying front doors.
- **Honest price mutability.** "Prices could change" stated on the launch
  whiteboard is the right disclosure posture: rates are parameters, not
  promises. Carry that into any future payout schedule as versioned,
  receipt-referenced pricing rather than marketing numbers.
- **The module registry as a post-R7 thesis, sharpened.** "Agentic npm" plus
  cryptographic verification plus payments is a genuinely differentiated
  registry design. When it returns, its unit is not a package but a verified
  program with a replay proof and a settlement history — the six-rung ladder
  applied to artifacts instead of assignments.
- **The 10,000-developers critique is the durable economics.** Redundant
  inference on identical functions is real waste. A registry of verified,
  composable computation is a coherent attack on it. That argument outlives
  Tassadar and does not require the LLM-computer substrate to be true.

## 4. How to make it come true

1. **Keep the verification wall load-bearing (active).** The loop's VERIFIED
   gate is already generalized: every fleet assignment closes through the
   six rungs, with exact-or-not_measured usage and body-free receipts.
   Acceptance: no work claim advances a rung without its evidence ref, and
   closeouts reconcile rather than assume.
2. **Keep Pylon the claim-execute-prove engine (active).** The contributor
   runtime carries the loop's shape for coding work today — typed requests,
   leases bounded by advertised capacity, exact `token_usage_events` rows,
   owner-only traces. Live-proven heterogeneous fleet work (the open
   simultaneous Codex+Claude proof, #8640) is the current equivalent of "the
   run is live."
3. **Hold the payment rail warm but decoupled (active).** BOLT12 settled
   receipts and the treasury wallet stay proven-but-idle for own-capacity
   work. Precondition for any paid loop: settlement rows reconcile to
   receipts, and payout claims are typed, never implied by counters.
4. **Post-R7 revisit gate: the module registry (bounded, no invented issue
   numbers).** After R0–R7 close, an owner decision can open a bounded leaf
   for a verified-artifact registry: typed module contracts, replay-based
   verification, provenance receipts, and settlement per use. Preconditions:
   the receipt ladder accepted as the artifact schema, and the no-resale
   invariant restated for whatever capacity executes registry work.
5. **If Tassadar-class training returns,** it re-enters as a consumer of the
   matured machinery — assignments, replay verification, exact settlement —
   with every sats figure reconciled to rows, and its world-first claims
   stated with proof rungs attached.

## 5. Risks and tensions

- **World-first crowing versus the claim protocol.** The raid-guild register
  is fun, but "first AI training run paid in Bitcoin" is a rung-four claim
  at best and was made without a public falsifier. The episode's own closing
  line ("no big claims that are not sourced by evidence") is in tension with
  its own headline. Current governance resolves that tension in favor of the
  closing line.
- **The 5K/5-sats discrepancy is a small parable.** Board and narration state
  different payout numbers. Under exact accounting that ambiguity cannot
  exist: the rate is whatever the settlement rows say, and any public number
  must be a projection of them. Counter movement is never proof.
- **Replay economics can be gamed.** Worker/validator pairs can collude, and
  a validator paid per verification is incentivized toward cheap approvals.
  Exact execution helps (replay is decidable), but any revived paid loop
  needs adversarial assignment of validators and slashing-grade consequences
  before real money scales.
- **The flywheel assumes demand that was never proven.** VERIFIED WORK →
  BETTER MODEL → LOWER COST → MORE DEMAND has an unpriced third arrow: no
  buy side existed. Episode 239 immediately tries to supply one, and the
  current roadmap's answer is stricter — prove the work rail on own capacity
  before pricing anything.
- **Vocabulary drift.** Tassadar lanes are retired. Pylon persists as the
  fleet/contributor runtime, not a training-node product. The marketplace is
  out of scope during R0–R7. Cite this episode for the replay-verification
  lineage, the agents.md front door, and the module-registry thesis — not
  for its product nouns, its payout rates, or its "live" tense.
