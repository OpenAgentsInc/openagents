## 1. What the episode says

This is a one-minute visual demo, self-aware about its own escalation: "Folks, I
might have gone a little bit overboard here." The host wanted to visualize the
Tassadar distributed training run that had been running "a couple days," and
each design step compounds on camera as a chain of "I may as well":

- The run already has "all these great API endpoints for agents," so "let's
  make a cool way to visualize it."
- "Alright, we may as well make it 3D."
- "I may as well be able to like walk around in it and see stuff."
- "I may as well be able to, you know, jump and sprint. And I may as well be
  able to tab-target."
- "I may as well put it in like a *Snow Crash* world" — a dark street of
  blocky building silhouettes with stats mounted along it.
- "And I may as well just add multiplayer, okay? So I added that, have not
  tested it."

The screen carries the substance the narration skips. The HUD shows a "92,654
sats" counter and the Autopilot title. A "Tassadar Run Board" panel reports
"Tassadar is active: 11 pylons, 6 active," 21 active windows, 12 verified work
items, 1,020 sats paid, with counters reading 5 DAYS / 21 WINDOWS / 12 VERIFIED
/ 1,020 ACTIVE SATS. A `net_gradient.demo` table shows training iterations,
loss, and base values. In the 3D world, floating boards display "Tassadar Run
Board" and "Status: active". Diamond and pylon-shaped markers carry labels like
"Tassadar," "My Pylon Base," and "assignment". A grey humanoid avatar walks,
runs, and jumps through the space. A bottom ticker scrolls blocker-grade refs in
plain sight: "REFS Tassadar training run core - waiting for public projection,"
"REFS blockers: 0 - refs: none," "REFS assignment waiting for public run - refs:
none."

The stated motivation is the durable part: "rather than just sit there and watch
your pylon stats, you know, how about let's have some fun with this?" The
episode closes as an open question rather than a commitment: "we might keep
evolving this. What do you think? See ya soon."

## 2. Where it stands against Revision 31

- **The subject of the board is retired.** Tassadar distributed training and
  its Bitcoin-paid run loops were retired/postponed from the active openagents
  plan in the 2026-07-08 docs-direction cleanup. The lanes the board
  visualized — pylons contributing training windows, verified work items, sats
  paid per window — are not in the current P0, which is strictly reliable
  Desktop/mobile coding plus fleet software. The machine-work economy the
  counters gestured at remains long-horizon vision, not present-tense product.
- **The world's backend is stranded by the platform exit.** The walkable-world
  direction later got a real Cloudflare-era backend (`apps/openagents-world`,
  Region Durable Objects, `packages/world-contract`, `packages/world-client`),
  but the workspace exited Cloudflare on 2026-07-06 and evacuated to GCP/Cloud
  Run. That backend predates the evacuation. No walkable 3D surface is in the
  current plan, and visual-polish/presentation programs are closed wontfix
  during R0–R7 (the glass/lowering closures #8646/#8650 are the precedent).
  `three-effect` remains the owned Three.js layer for any future visualization
  return.
- **The supervision instinct came true in typed form.** What the board wanted
  — run status, capacity, verified work, blockers, and payment at a glance,
  live — is what shipped as typed machinery: FC-3 #8639 (pause/resume, exact
  steer, exact approval, body-free receipts, one-minute closeout card),
  Desktop D5 as the authoritative Fleet cockpit, and decision 22's agent
  topology as a live operating surface with "persistent topology and
  low-latency direct control, not decorative density" as the explicit goal.
  Episodes 248/249 then turned discovery and topology into executable release
  contracts, and #8674/#8675 landed the loss-accounted history workspace.
- **The ticker was ahead of its time.** "Blockers: 0 - refs: none" and
  "waiting for public projection" are the refs-and-blockers vocabulary the
  current receipt and closeout machinery formalizes. The demo was already
  speaking in typed status refs, just rendered on a virtual street.
- **Multiplayer presence is deferred, and its on-camera status was honest.**
  "So I added that, have not tested it" is, in six-rung vocabulary, code-landed
  at best — and the episode said so out loud. No presence surface is in the
  current P0. The multiplayer/Verse direction waits with the rest of the
  visualization program.
- **The exactness culture had not yet arrived.** The board's counters (12
  verified, 1,020 sats, 92,654 sats) predate the exact-or-not_measured usage
  regime and the rule that counter movement is never proof. Under current
  governance, every number on such a board would have to be a projection of
  exact rows, reconcilable on demand.

## 3. What to extract and carry forward

- **Make work watchable — extract the instinct, drop the 3D requirement.** The
  episode's real claim is that fleet state should be legible and even
  enjoyable to observe, not that it needs an avatar. That instinct survives
  intact as decision 22's persistent-topology supervision goal and the D5
  Fleet cockpit: complete roster, causal inline child activity, independent
  child transcripts, direct control. The "StarCraft" ambition is already
  scoped correctly in decision 22's own words — persistent topology and
  low-latency direct control, "not decorative density."
- **The board's information design is worth keeping.** One status line, a
  capacity fraction (11 pylons / 6 active), a verified-work count, a payment
  total, day/window counters, and a scrolling refs-and-blockers ticker is a
  genuinely good one-glance layout for a run surface. The current equivalents
  — closeout cards, receipts, the loss-accounted history workspace — should
  preserve that density: one glance answers "is it alive, how much capacity,
  what is proven, what is blocked, what did it cost."
- **Presence as a supervision primitive, not avatars.** The multiplayer
  impulse maps to a real future need: multi-operator visibility over one
  fleet. When it returns, it should be typed presence over the topology
  surface (who is watching, who holds steer/approval authority), not spatial
  embodiment. The retained `world-contract`/`world-client` packages are the
  historical schema source for public-safe presence rows if ever revived.
- **"Fun" as a retention property, bounded by the wontfix gate.** "Rather than
  just sit there and watch your pylon stats" names something true: operators
  supervise longer when the surface rewards attention. During R0–R7 the fun
  budget goes to cockpit latency and legibility. Post-R7, `three-effect` is
  the designated home if 3D returns.
- **The "I may as well" chain as a cautionary pattern.** The episode documents
  scope compounding in real time — five escalations from "visualize the run"
  to "untested multiplayer in a Snow Crash world" in under a minute. Current
  governance (bounded leaves, the claim protocol, wontfix gates on
  presentation work) exists precisely to make that chain require a decision at
  each link.
- **Honest status on camera.** The demo volunteered its own weakest claim
  ("have not tested it") and rendered its blockers in the ticker. That instinct
  predates and matches the claim protocol. Preserve it in every future demo.

## 4. How to make it come true

The honest restatement of "make it come true" is: make the *supervision value*
real under the current roadmap, and give the *spatial form* a defined revisit
gate.

1. **Land the typed board (active).** The board's content contract is carried
   by closed FC-3 #8639 (receipts, one-minute closeout card) and the open
   Desktop ladder, where D5 makes Desktop the authoritative Fleet cockpit.
   Acceptance in six-rung vocabulary: the cockpit shows live roster,
   capacity, blockers, and cost from receipt projections, live-proven on a
   real fleet run, then owner-accepted.
2. **Prove liveness on real work (active).** #8640's live simultaneous
   Codex+Claude proof supplies the demo content the 2026 board lacked:
   heterogeneous real assignments instead of a `net_gradient.demo` table,
   with every number sourced from exact `token_usage_events` and assignment
   rows.
3. **Topology as the render substrate (active).** Decision 22 plus the
   episodes-248/249 release contracts (#8674/#8675 landed) define the data
   surface any future visualization renders from. No parallel world-state
   store: a future 3D view is a renderer over the same typed topology, or it
   does not exist.
4. **Post-R7 revisit gate for the spatial form (bounded, no invented issue
   number).** After R0–R7 close, an owner decision can reopen a walkable or
   spatial view built on `three-effect`, fed exclusively by topology/receipt
   projections, with presence expressed as typed operator sessions.
   Preconditions: D5 accepted as the supervision authority. The visualization
   ships read-only over receipts. Any multiplayer presence passes its own
   fixture-proven rung before a live demo — no repeat of "have not tested it"
   as a shipped claim.
5. **If Tassadar-class training ever returns,** its run board is a projection
   of the same receipt machinery, with sats-paid figures reconciled to
   settlement rows — the 2026 counters, but exact, and each public number
   traceable to its rows.

## 5. Risks and tensions

- **Spatial spectacle as progress proof.** The board looked like a running
  economy. The ticker admitted the core run was "waiting for public
  projection." A beautiful world rendered over unproven work inverts the
  receipts-over-opaque-state invariant. Never let the render outrun the rows.
- **Reviving the world backend out of nostalgia.** The Cloudflare-era world
  apps and packages still exist in the tree, but the platform exit and the
  R0–R7 wontfix gate both stand between them and production. Reviving them
  before the gate is a policy violation, not initiative.
- **Presentation work displacing P0.** The episode's charm is exactly the
  temptation Revision 31 guards against: visualization is cheap dopamine
  relative to portable-session reliability and fleet-software correctness.
  During R0–R7 the answer to "we might keep evolving this" is: not yet, and
  not as spectacle.
- **Untested features as demo habit.** Shipping "added, have not tested" on
  camera was fine for a prototype vlog. Under the claim protocol it is a
  rung-one claim at most. Future demos of supervision surfaces should state
  their proof rung explicitly rather than letting the footage imply more.
- **Sats counters without settlement truth.** The 92,654-sats HUD and
  1,020-sats-paid figures predate exact accounting. Any revived money-adjacent
  display must reconcile to settlement rows, and marketplace resale remains
  out of scope — owner-local subscription capacity is never resold.
- **Vocabulary drift.** "Autopilot" as a product name is retired from the
  active plan. Tassadar lanes are retired. Pylon persists but as the
  contributor/fleet runtime, not a training-window contributor. Cite this
  episode for the watchability instinct and the board's information design —
  not for its product nouns or its numbers.
