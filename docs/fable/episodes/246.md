## 1. What the episode says

The host goes full-time inside Khala Code as his only coding harness: "stop
using Codex, just force yourself to fix the bullshit." The episode is a live
dogfooding session — full-screen desktop app, wrapping Codex with Claude Code
support being added — in which every UX complaint encountered on camera
("Invalid session id", missing chat titles, a phantom "current chat" label, an
inert "Plan" button, wrong spinner semantics, a vanished active-chat background
color) is immediately converted into a work order dispatched to the app itself.
Failures surface real requirements on camera: exhausted-account errors must be
surfaced as the actual error class, and dispatch "should automatically route to
the next one" of the connected Codex accounts.

The structural idea of the episode is the "UX Behavior Contract": the
micro-interaction equivalent of product promises. The registry is described
precisely — "every entry records the statement verbatim, who stated it and
where, and the oracle tests that enforce it in the normal test suite," with a
paired coverage test that "fails the sweep if an enforced contract loses its
oracle, so stated behavior cannot silently drift," a human rendering kept in
sync, and the rule that "telling a session is recording it, that is the point of
this file." Enforcement requires at least one oracle asserting on real behavior,
an automated tier, and zero blocker refs — "the same mechanical green-gate
discipline as the product promise registry." Contract deviations found in the
wild are strict bugs.

The episode then demonstrates contract mining: an Opus/Sonnet session traverses
"the last 36 hours" of Codex and Claude conversation history — 43 in-app
sessions plus adjacent desktop and Claude sessions — and lands every stated
Khala Code UX expectation as a pending contract, converting "me screaming at my
voice transcription thing" into structured, verifiable contracts. The host
explicitly frames this as the future customer-intake flow: business customers
"vibing about what your problems are," converted into contracts, then software,
then payment.

A second product is sketched: QA Swarm — "point a swarm of QA agents at your
product and get proof it works," with scripted scenarios, seeded monkeys, LLM
explorers, and perf probes driving a real browser, terminal, and native window.
every discovery distilled into a committed re-runnable e2e test. Every run
producing "an honest CONFIRMED/REFUTED verdict, videos, exact accounting, and a
shareable web URL" rendered as a StarCraft-blue live board. "We are customer
number one. QA Swarm's standing engagement is Khala Code desktop itself, and its
first sales artifact is the evidence that engagement produces."

The episode names the win condition and the two-product frame: "The thing that I
need as a company is to close the gap between what I've been saying and what
we are shipping. That is it. If I close that gap, I have a successful company."
OpenAgents has two products — Khala Code (the Claude Code equivalent) and
Autopilot (the business system, with promise/contract rigor "applied all up and
down the business") — powered by parallelized fleets (the 2.4-billion-token
overnight run across six ChatGPT accounts via local Pylon) and a soon-to-close
revenue loop. Also stated: game UX over terminal UX ("I want to multiply
gamers"), stable rebindable hotkeys as muscle memory, diff visibility as
entertainment, and the forum pulled into the app as the beginning of the social
layer.

## 2. Where it stands against Revision 31

- **The UX Behavior Contract became repo law and outgrew its origin.** The
  2026-07-03 owner mandate generalizes exactly what this episode prototyped: any
  stated UX/product expectation lands in the owning surface's behavior-contract
  registry in the same change — statement verbatim plus oracle test — with the
  shared schema and coverage checker in `packages/behavior-contracts`. This is
  the episode's clearest "came true," and it came true at a higher altitude than
  the episode asked for: repo-wide rather than one client's registry. The
  specific registries shown on camera
  (`clients/khala-code-desktop/src/contracts/ux-contracts.ts`,
  `docs/khala-code/khala-code-ux-contract.md`) are now parity/migration inputs
  only, because —
- **Khala Code the client is deprecated.** `clients/khala-code-desktop` and
  `clients/khala-mobile` are frozen extraction sources. The successors are
  greenfield OpenAgents Desktop (`apps/openagents-desktop`, Electron + Effect
  Native, tokenless renderer behind a host-owned Runtime Gateway) and OpenAgents
  mobile (`apps/openagents-mobile`). The episode's dogfooding target no longer
  exists as a product. The dogfooding *discipline* transferred to the successors
  (R7 explicitly includes dogfood, CUT-01..CUT-27, #8681–#8707, carry the local
  coding cutover).
- **The two-product naming was superseded.** "Autopilot" is retired from the
  active plan as a product name. The fleet-of-coding-agents idea survives as
  FleetRun/Pylon/fleet cockpit (Desktop D5) and Khala fleet delegation (`khala
  fleet connect`, Artanis-as-a-Service). "Khala" itself narrowed to the
  collective-intelligence/inference/Sync engine names.
- **QA Swarm was never productized.** The June out-ship-the-factory QA epic
  (#6181) ran, and the current quality machinery is behavior contracts +
  deterministic verify gates + the six proof rungs (code-landed → fixture-proven
  → deployed → live-proven → owner-accepted → closed) +
  real-Electron/physical-device acceptance journeys (#8675 pattern). That
  machinery is internal law, not a sellable engagement.
- **Automatic account failover became real contract language.** The runbook now
  specifies typed `account_exhausted` / `account_rate_limited` classes and retry
  to the next ready connected Codex account — exactly the complaint from minute
  02:52.
- **The fleet pattern matured.** The episode's ad hoc "Codex app calling the
  Khala API delegating to six accounts" is now the documented Khala → Pylon →
  Codex delegation runbook with exact `token_usage_events` accounting and
  "counter movement is never proof." FC-1/2/3 closed. #8640 (Live simultaneous
  Codex+Claude proof) remains open — the episode's "Codex and soon Claude Code"
  is still the pending half.
- **The revenue-loop close and business-customer contract intake are deferred.**
  Commercial/web expansion is explicitly deferred during R0–R7. Web is
  maintenance-only. The customer-onboarding-as-contract-intake flow is pending,
  not superseded.

## 3. What to extract and carry forward

- **The say/ship gap as the single win condition.** This is the episode's most
  durable sentence and it is already institutional: the claim protocol,
  challenge ledger with falsifiers, product-promise registry, and six proof
  rungs are all machinery for measuring and closing exactly that gap. Any future
  strategy doc that needs a one-line company thesis should use this one.
- **Dogfood as forcing function.** "Force yourself to fix the bullshit" is the
  mechanism by which R7 (signed releases + dogfood) earns its place as the
  ladder's top rung. The episode demonstrates why the dogfooder must be the
  owner on the primary workflow, not a QA pass: only full-time use surfaces the
  phantom-label, spinner-semantics, hotkey-stability class of defect. Carry
  forward the operational rule: every live complaint lands as a registry entry
  in the same session, never as a memory.
- **Contract mining from session history.** The 36-hour mining pass is a
  genuinely novel pipeline: unstructured owner speech → verbatim statement →
  typed pending contract → oracle in a later code pass. It generalizes to (a)
  periodic sweeps of Codex/Claude session archives for unlanded expectations,
  and (b) the eventual customer-intake interview that converts a prospect's
  complaints into a ProductSpec plus behavior-contract set. The `specs/`
  ProductSpec format (v0.1) is the current home for the "what/why" half.
  behavior contracts are the enforcement half. The two-layer design shown on
  camera — machine registry plus human rendering kept in sync by a test — is
  exactly the `packages/behavior-contracts` shape and should be preserved in
  every new surface's registry.
- **Pending-with-blocker as a first-class state.** The episode's docs-first
  mining pass ("just do the docs, we'll do the actual code pass afterward")
  legitimizes `pending` entries with blocker refs as honest intermediate state
  rather than vaporware — provided the coverage test keeps them visible. That
  maps to rung one (code-landed) of six being explicitly not the end.
- **QA Swarm's verdict vocabulary, not its productization.** CONFIRMED/REFUTED
  verdicts, exact accounting, receipts-light-the-edges visualization: those
  concepts survive inside the challenge ledger, deterministic verify gates, and
  acceptance journeys. The "every discovery distills into a committed
  re-runnable e2e test" rule is worth adopting verbatim inside the #8675
  acceptance-journey pattern.
- **Game UX, stable hotkeys, perceived speed as contract subjects.** "Hotkeys
  are supposed to be muscle memory," screen-to-screen latency budgets, and
  legible live activity (a diff viewer that "makes some freaking sense") are
  Desktop D0–D6 concerns and should enter the OpenAgents Desktop
  behavior-contract registry as measurable entries, not vibes.

## 4. How to make it come true

- **Already carried:** the behavior-contract mandate (repo law since 2026-07-03,
  schema in `packages/behavior-contracts`). Typed account-health failover
  (delegation runbook). Fleet substrate FC-1/2/3 closed. The Desktop D0–D6 and
  CUT-01..CUT-27 (#8681–#8707) lanes carry the "solid coding-agent cockpit"
  ambition into the greenfield client. R7 institutionalizes dogfood.
- **Parity migration:** a bounded leaf under the Desktop D-ladder should port
  every still-relevant entry from the frozen Khala Code registries into the
  OpenAgents Desktop registry — statement verbatim, provenance preserved,
  oracles rewritten against the Electron/Effect Native surface. Acceptance:
  coverage test green in the normal sweep (code-landed → fixture-proven). The
  frozen registries then need no further maintenance.
- **Mining as a standing sweep:** a bounded new leaf (post-R5, when Desktop is
  the daily harness) could schedule a recurring session-history mining pass that
  proposes pending contracts as PRs. Acceptance is fixture-proven: the sweep
  produces verbatim-sourced pending entries and the coverage checker holds them
  visible until oracles land.
- **Simultaneous multi-harness proof:** #8640 (live simultaneous Codex+Claude)
  is the open issue that completes the episode's "Codex and soon Claude Code"
  promise. Its closure at live-proven rung is the honest checkpoint.
- **Perceived-speed and hotkey-stability contracts:** bounded leaves under the
  Desktop epic adding measurable oracles (navigation latency budget,
  hotkey-binding stability across list reorder) to the Desktop registry, proven
  in real-Electron acceptance journeys per the #8675 pattern.
- **Customer contract intake:** post-R7 revisit gate. When commercial work
  reopens, the intake interview → ProductSpec → behavior-contract pipeline is
  the design to build, with the promise registry's green-gate discipline as the
  acceptance bar. Do not build it during R0–R7.

## 5. Risks and tensions

- **Do not resurrect Khala Code.** The episode's affection for the specific
  client is the biggest trap. Every "make it like the video" instinct must
  target `apps/openagents-desktop`, and the frozen clients receive no features.
  "Khala Code" as a name should not reappear in new product copy.
- **QA Swarm as a sellable product conflicts with the R0–R7 commercial freeze.**
  Selling QA engagements before the company's own say/ship gap is closed would
  recreate the gap one level up. The internal machinery is the extraction. The
  product is at most a post-R7 revisit.
- **The two-product frame is stale vocabulary.** "Autopilot: business operating
  system" language must not leak into current docs or copy. The fleet idea lives
  under FleetRun/Pylon/fleet-cockpit names.
- **Mining passes that commit with `--no-verify` and push to main overnight** —
  as done on camera — are exactly the pattern the claim protocol, verify gates,
  and six rungs now exist to discipline. Carry the mining idea, not the
  2026-06-era looseness. Duplicate PRs and agents stepping on each other were
  acknowledged costs in the episode itself.
- **Contract volume vs. oracle debt.** A registry that accumulates pending
  entries faster than oracles land inverts the tool into a wish list. The
  coverage checker keeps drift visible, but sequencing discipline (Revision 31,
  not registry order) decides what gets enforced when.
- **Bitcoin-wallet/social-layer bundling claims** ("no one's going to compete
  with all of that") are promise-registry material, not assumed features. Any
  public restatement must route through `docs/promises/` and carry green gates.
