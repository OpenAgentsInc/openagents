## 1. What the episode says

The episode is a working session on one question: "what is the ideal sub-agent chat? Like what do I actually want to see?" It opens with a comparative indictment. In the Codex TUI, sub-agents like `roadmap_audit` appear but "I have no way of like easily checking in on those"; Claude Code "at least gives me like some arrow keys"; and the closed ChatGPT/Codex desktop app has the topology but hides the history ("Like how do I get to this chat?"). The complaint is not missing data — it is missing *navigable structure*.

The methodological centerpiece is the teardown corpus at `docs/teardowns/`: "point-in-time, read-only teardowns of installed products and commit-pinned open-source implementations... The goal is not feature admiration or pixel copying. Each teardown separates observed evidence from inference then asks which boundaries, workflows, and failure modes should change OpenAgents' product decisions."

From the synthesis doc he reads the eight adaptation decisions on camera: web-capable desktop shell (not the agent engine); real agent engine outside the renderer; versioned query/command/event stream between UI and engine; local tools, MCP, skills, plugins, native capabilities; separate isolation for untrusted code execution; desktop/mobile continuity and remote steering; independently updateable components; and "a workbench that grows beyond chat without making every conversation look like an IDE." Electron is chosen by convergence, not preference: "if OpenAI, Anthropic, and OpenCode have all converged on Electron for desktop app, like that's it... We can get fancy or cute later on."

The Codex-specific teardown supplies the technical claim: "Codex has a real first-class multi-agent system" — a parent thread can `spawn_agent`, `send_input`, `send_message`, `wait`, `resume_agent`, `close_agent`, with "10 collaboration-specific variants" in the core event vocabulary — but "the gap is a rendering/topology gap, structural to a terminal. A terminal is a singular linear scrollback showing one thread's transcript at a time... Sub-agent structure is therefore flattened into three degraded forms." His verdict is blunt: "Get the f*** out of the terminal... Give me buttons... let me see what... is going on, okay? Make it clicky. It's got to be clicky. It's got to feel like StarCraft... RTS."

The demo shows the OpenAgents Desktop answer taking shape: a left sidebar of Codex history, a main transcript with custom components per agent-message type ("inter-agent handoff," "task assigned," terminal blocks), and a right-sidebar roster — "Agents - 131" — that graduates from generic "Subagent" rows to Codex's auto-assigned names (Pascal, Gibbs, Leibniz, Halley, Averroes, "Heisenberg delegating to Sagan"). Clicking an agent message opens a detail view with `agent_id`, `operation: sub_agent_activity`, and the originating task.

Two concrete UX requests are stated: when a sub-agent spawns, "let me click into it from there," and the spawn card should "show some of the like latest message of the sub-agent in that box," so the parent transcript conveys child progress causally inline. The close names the residue explicitly: "The next question is like what information do I actually want from these agents?... next get the full streaming hooking up. I want to get clicky with it... I got my hotkeys going now and just see how far we can take this into StarCraft land."

## 3. What to extract and carry forward

1. **Topology is data, rendering is choice — and the terminal chose wrong.**
   The episode's sharpest finding is that Codex's engine already carries full parent-child structure and the TUI *destroys* it structurally.
   In current vocabulary: the typed protocol must carry Work Unit topology end-to-end, and every read surface renders it loss-accounted rather than flattened. "Flattened into three degraded forms" is the negative space that justifies the completeness equation.

2. **The three simultaneous views.**
   Roster (complete list with status), causal inline spawn cards in the parent transcript, and independent child transcripts one click deep.
   This triple is now law 28–30 material; the episode is its clearest narrative articulation and should anchor any future redesign debate — a surface that drops one of the three views regresses the contract.

3. **Spawn cards as live projections, not log lines.**
   "Show some of the latest message of the sub-agent in that box" defines the inline card as a bounded window onto the child's stream — the parent transcript stays causal (spawn appears where it happened) while the card carries fresh child state.
   This is the precise seam where the durable log and the volatile stream meet in one component, and it is the design target for the live-graph follow-ons.

4. **Evidence-vs-inference teardown discipline.**
   The corpus's stated method — separate observed evidence from inference, ask which *boundaries and failure modes* should change our decisions, refuse pixel copying — is a reusable research contract.
   It already produced the convergent architecture and the rejection list; keep applying it before any new surface is designed, and keep teardowns commit-pinned and read-only.

5. **Convergence as an engineering-risk heuristic.**
   The Electron decision ("that's it, we're just going to do the same thing") is a general rule: where three independent competent vendors converge, adopt the convergent choice and spend novelty budget on the actual differentiator — typed contracts, receipts, loss-accounting — not the shell. "We can get fancy or cute later on."

6. **RTS as an interaction contract, not a skin.**
   "Clicky... StarCraft... RTS" decodes to: many concurrent units, one commander; every unit selectable; selection reveals state instantly; every command issuable by pointer or hotkey with identical semantics.
   That is exactly law 28–30's pointer/keyboard/palette parity, and it extends naturally to FleetRun steer/approve controls: supervising a fleet should feel like commanding units, with authority always flowing through typed intents.

7. **Identity for ephemeral workers.**
   The delight at named agents ("Heisenberg delegating to Sagan") is a real usability observation: stable human-readable handles make a 131-agent roster scannable and delegation chains narratable.
   Carry the handle as projection metadata attached to the Work Unit — a display affordance, never an authority or persona surface.

## 4. How to make it come true

The persisted-history half is closed; the residue is liveness, portability, input parity, and the small screen:

- **Live supervision (the "full streaming" residue).**
  The follow-on live-graph work in the #8691/#8692-class space should bind the volatile stream to the shipped topology: roster rows and spawn cards update while children run, with the durable log remaining the authority the stream reconciles against.
  Acceptance in six-rung terms: fixture-proven with a replayed multi-child event stream; live-proven with a real running FleetRun where a child's latest message appears in the parent's spawn card before the child completes, receipted against the durable log.
- **Fleet-grade topology through FC-5.**
  #8640's accepted simultaneous Codex+Claude FleetRun is the natural corpus for proving the topology surface across providers and named isolated accounts: the roster must show both engines' workers under one parent authority, loss-accounted, with rejected-closeout repair history visible rather than smoothed over.
  A bounded leaf in the FC follow-on space would assert the completeness equation over a mixed-provider FleetRun projection.
- **Portable graph fencing.**
  Under the product sentence (stop/checkpoint/rehydrate across authorized hosts, not live migration), the parent-child graph must survive a session move without forking identity or receipts: children fence at checkpoint, and rehydration renders the same roster with explicit gaps for anything the checkpoint could not carry.
  This is a bounded leaf riding the R2 Khala Sync rung and the CUT-01..CUT-27 graph, with acceptance being a checkpoint/rehydrate cycle whose before/after topology satisfies the completeness equation.
- **Input parity oracles.**
  Laws 28–30 need executable behavior-contract oracles asserting that every roster/spawn-card/child-transcript intent is reachable by pointer, hotkey, and palette with identical typed-intent payloads — enforced in the normal sweep (fixture-proven) and demonstrated in real Electron (live-proven).
- **Mobile tap parity.**
  R6 and the M-gates owe the compact rendering of the same topology: roster as a drill-in list, spawn cards tappable, child transcripts full-screen — same contracts, same completeness equation, receipted with physical-device pixel proof per the standing rule.
  That would also let the #8676-class continuity journey include a fleet-supervision scene rather than a single-thread chat only.
- **Khala Sync as the topology transport.**
  For remote supervision (Desktop watching a FleetRun executing on another authorized host), the roster projection should arrive over Khala Sync scopes rather than a bespoke channel, keeping the Runtime Gateway host-owned and the renderer tokenless.

## 5. Risks and tensions

- **Live streams must not become authority.**
  Wiring "full streaming" invites rendering volatile events as truth. The standing rejection of transcript-as-authority applies doubly to in-flight child streams: the durable log and receipts remain authoritative; the stream is a projection that reconciles or shows an explicit gap.
  "A timeout is not an outcome" governs stalled children — a silent spawn card must display an honest unknown-state, never a fake "running."
- **StarCraft framing can drift into spectacle.**
  The RTS metaphor earns its keep as an input-parity and legibility contract. A literal game-render of agents would burn effort the reliability ladder (R0–R7) needs and prove nothing. Keep "clicky" bound to typed intents and latency, not cosmetics.
- **Named agents are not personas.**
  Post-#8610 ("all sarah shit must die"), Pascal-and-Heisenberg naming must stay display metadata on Work Units.
  No agent name may accrete a voice, avatar, or front-door surface; the narrowly reauthorized conversational-voice lane is persona-neutral, session-neutral, and never authority by itself.
- **The roster must not become ambient surveillance.**
  "Let me see what's going on" is scoped to work the owner authorized, projected loss-accounted.
  The teardown rejections (no ambient screen memory) and the tokenless-renderer boundary must hold as the roster goes live and cross-host: the renderer never gains direct filesystem or credential reach just to make a card fresher.
- **Vendor topology is borrowed ground.**
  The spawn/handoff event vocabulary rendered here is Codex's unversioned internal shape; Claude's differs and Grok's is unproven (postponed on quota/402).
  The projection layer must normalize into the owned Thread→Turn→Item + Work Unit/Receipt vocabulary, with unknown variants surfacing as explicit gaps — otherwise a vendor event rename silently falsifies the roster, and truthful green (R0) rots without a failing oracle to say so.
- **Do not let the desktop app become the only truthful window.**
  The episode's disgust at the TUI should not license abandoning non-GUI surfaces. The contract is that *every* read surface is honest about topology at its own fidelity — a CLI can render a loss-accounted summary with explicit flattening markers.
  The failure being rejected is silent degradation, not text itself.
