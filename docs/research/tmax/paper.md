## Abstract

Terminal-using agents have quickly become the most popular downstream
application of language models. Despite their prevalence, relatively little
*academic* work has examined RL-based training of these models — due to
difficult benchmarks, a lack of data, and a lack of simple baseline recipes.
**TMAX** is presented as the strongest open RL recipe for terminal agents to
date, bringing open-data recipes closer to the frontier. Though simple, it
achieves **27% on Terminal-Bench 2.0 with only 9B parameters**, outperforming
much larger models from prior open work. The data is generated with a novel
taxonomy combining difficulty control, personas, and verifier diversification,
which cheaply produces large amounts of terminal environments for RL and SFT.
The released terminal dataset is **>2.5× larger** than previously released
terminal-agent datasets. Models are then trained with RL on this data using a
**simple, outcome-only recipe**. Data, models, and code are released as a strong
baseline for future open academic work.

## 1. Introduction

Terminal-based agentic coding products have become extremely popular, with
models expected to perform increasingly complex, long-running tasks through the
terminal. But existing academic work has largely focused on bug-fixing (SWE-Bench
style) repository tasks; terminal agents — which manage their own history through
a harness and use general bash tooling — are under-studied in the open.

Two obstacles: (1) **data** — building RL environments for terminal tasks is
hard (containerized, programmatically verifiable, difficulty-balanced); (2) **a
simple, reproducible recipe** that small teams can actually run and build on.
TMAX addresses both.

Headline contributions:
- **TMAX-15K**, a dataset of ~14,600 RL environments (≈2.5× larger than prior
  open terminal datasets), generated by a compositional taxonomy.
- **TMAX models** (2B / 4B / 9B / 27B) trained with a simple outcome-only RL
  recipe; TMAX-9B hits ~27% on Terminal-Bench 2.0, beating larger open-recipe
  models and dominating the Pareto curve under 32B parameters (Figure 1).
- A **reproducible recipe** — data, algorithm (DPPO + FP32 LM head), codebase
  (open-instruct fork), and documented pitfalls — released in full.

## 2. Background and Related Work

- **2.1 Data for terminal agents.** Prior work yields synthetic SFT data or
  smaller/less-diverse, less-difficulty-balanced RL sets (e.g. Endless Terminals,
  OpenThinker-Agent). TMAX targets a larger, difficulty-aware, well-balanced
  corpus across terminal task families.
- **2.2 RL training for terminal agents.** RLVR-style training is well explored
  on math; terminal-agent RL is comparatively immature in the open. TMAX
  positions itself as a clean, stable baseline recipe rather than a new
  algorithm.

## 3. Terminal Data Generation

Training autonomous terminal agents requires large-scale, diverse coding tasks.
TMAX's pipeline (Figure 2) is **simple, scalable, diverse, and difficulty-aware**.

### 3.1 Generation pipeline — compositional sampling

A task is sampled as an **independent product of structured (orthogonal) axes**,
then instantiated into a self-contained Docker/Apptainer environment with a
**programmatic verifier** and unit tests, solved at pass@k by a mini-SWE-agent
harness. Axes include (Figure 2): **domain/skills**, **difficulty**, **persona**,
**language**, **CLI complexity**, **task category**, **failure modes**, etc. The
generator (Gemini-3-Pro) composes axis signatures, pre-builds a per-domain base
image, and builds each task on top — so a single base image amortizes across
many tasks, keeping generation cheap and scalable.

- **Scalability via soft filtering.** Unlike prior work that filters out tasks an
  agent can't currently solve (no learning signal), TMAX deliberately *keeps*
  hard tasks: difficulty is a controlled axis, not a discard filter.
- **Diversity via hierarchical sampling.** The compositional sampler is the main
  diversity mechanism; drawing axes from independent product space avoids the
  collapse seen when tasks are generated free-form.
- **Persona diversification** and **verifier diversification** further widen the
  corpus and reduce the risk of a single exploitable reward signal. Personas are
  domain-specific (5–18 per domain) — e.g. "red-team operator crafting an evasion
  payload" — so security tasks are always reasonable.
- **Multi-modal / intricate tasks.** Newer tasks perceive these artifacts
  through standard terminal tooling (OCR, audio translation, ffmpeg), so no
  multi-modal model is required.

### 3.1 Difficulty calibration & balance

- **Fine-grained complexity.** Difficulty is an explicit effort to control the
  data distribution, addressing two mechanisms: command count (30–60 commands)
  and *command complexity* (chained commands, system services, granular control).
- **Graded verifiers.** Verifiers are difficulty-aware: thresholds give partial
  credit, with optional per-bucket up-weights to match a target difficulty
  distribution (a curriculum), naturally extending task length.
- **Balance score.** A scalar `Balance = exp(H)/N` (with `H` the Shannon entropy
  of the bucket proportions `pᵢ` and `N` the number of buckets) measures how
  uniformly tasks spread across difficulty/skill buckets. TMAX scores high
  (≈0.95+ domain, ≈0.72 difficulty) versus much lower for prior datasets.
- **De-contamination.** Overlap with Terminal-Bench / SWE-bench checked via
  3-gram sliding window; reported low (sub-1%) and lower than several prior sets.

### 3.3 SFT data generation

A small SFT warm-start set is generated from the RL tasks (used to study whether
SFT warm-starting helps). ~16.5K SFT trajectories are produced from ~5K
environments; these are *successful* trajectories for SFT, and the harder
environments are reserved for the RL phase.

### 3.4 Agent harness — "vanillux"

A deliberately **simple** harness derived from mini-SWE-agent: bash-tool schema,
a submit marker, format-error recovery, output truncation, executed through
Harbor's active environment. Simplicity is a stated *feature* — easier for small
teams, and it reduces complexity during RL training (versus heavy industry
harnesses). Trade-off: scores may be lower than possible SOTA, accepted in
exchange for reproducibility and accessibility.

## 4. Experiments

### 4.1 Setup

- **Algorithm — DPPO.** Models are trained with **DPPO (Qi et al., 2026)**, a
  variant of GRPO that **masks tokens when inference and training logprobs
  deviate** — specifically masking based on a *binary approximation of the total
  variation (TV) divergence* between the two distributions (token-level).
  Divergence threshold `0.1`.
- **FP32 LM head.** The LM head is computed and kept in **FP32** to minimize
  the **training–inference mismatch** (following MiniMax 2025). This is found
  *especially crucial* for Qwen 3.5. Figure 4 plots the maximum logprob
  difference between the vLLM inference engine and the HF trainer during RL; the
  FP32 head sharply reduces it (high-frequency tokens, e.g. `\n`, drive the worst
  mismatch).
- **Base models.** Qwen 3.5 dense (2B / 4B / 9B) and Qwen3-27B; trained into
  TMAX-2B/4B/9B/27B.
- **Key RL hyperparameters (Table 13):** bfloat16 weights, FP32 LM head, gradient
  checkpointing; max prompt 2048, per-turn max 16384, max total response 65536
  (32768 for Qwen 3 8B); **8 unique prompts/rollout batch, group size 32**, max
  async steps 4, active sampling on, **filter zero-std samples**, temperature
  1.0; AdamW, **500 training steps**, LR `1e-6` constant, max grad norm 1.0;
  **DPPO loss, centered advantage normalization, KL β = 0.0, binary-TV divergence,
  threshold 0.1**; max tool/env steps 64, bash timeout 120s.
- **Eval.** Terminal-Bench 2.0 (and TB-Lite / TB 2.1), plus SWE-Bench Verified
  and AIME for generalization; all models served on a single A100 node with vLLM,
  each eval averaged over 3 runs. Daytona (cloud) and Podman (local) sandboxes
  used; small score discrepancies between them are noted.
- **Infrastructure.** A standard training job is **8 nodes of H100s (2 train, 6
  inference) for 2–3 days**; establishing the recipe took **O(100)** such jobs.

### 4.2 Core results

- **TMAX-15K outperforms other terminal datasets.** RL on TMAX-15K beats RL on
  prior open terminal datasets (TermGen, Endless Terminals, OpenThinker-Agent,
  TerminalTraj, CLI-Gym, SWE-Smith) on Terminal-Bench Lite and TB 2.1 (Table 2),
  using an identical harness and base model — isolating the *data* as the cause.
- **TMAX-9B is strong for its size.** ~27% on Terminal-Bench 2.0 at 9B,
  outperforming much larger open-recipe models and dominating the sub-32B Pareto
  frontier; shoulder-to-shoulder region with substantially larger models
  (Figure 1).
- **RL improves across model sizes (Table 3).** TMAX RL training improves over
  the base model at every size tested (2B → 27B); e.g. roughly a doubling at the
  small end on TB-Lite, and consistent gains up to 27B.
- **Generalizes to other tasks (Table 4).** TMAX-9B RL also improves SWE-Bench
  Verified and AIME 24/25 over base — evidence the training teaches general
  capability, not just Terminal-Bench-specific behavior.

### 4.3 Generalization

TMAX RL improves on tasks and model families beyond the training distribution
(other terminal benchmarks, SWE-bench, AIME; Qwen 3 8B as well as Qwen 3.5),
indicating the recipe is not narrowly overfit.

## 5. Challenges in training TMAX-9B (the pitfalls)

This is the "recipe" half — the documented failure modes and stabilizers.

- **5.1 Qwen 3.5 doesn't benefit from existing SFT datasets.** Common
  SFT-warm-start-then-RL recipes did not help: Qwen 3.5 is already heavily
  trained on the task, so existing SFT data underperforms a larger, harder
  RL-data mixture run directly. (Tables 5–6 study SFT vs no-SFT and the FP32
  head's effect.)
- **Training–inference mismatch is the central instability.** The FP32 LM head
  (above) is the primary fix; without it, RL on Qwen 3.5 is unstable.
- **DPPO limits training collapse (Figure 7).** Compared head-to-head with GRPO
  (OpenInstruct GRPO using vLLM logprobs as π_old, clip-higher 0.272 / clip-lower
  0.2), **DPPO is more stable** — token-level masking on logprob divergence
  prevents the reward collapse GRPO exhibits. Group-size ablation (Figure 8)
  varies group sizes (8, 16, 32).
- **Infra dependence & "awareness."** To keep costs low, sandbox containers
  (Podman) were run on the same nodes as inference, causing resource contention
  and sandbox-management bottlenecks. **Anecdotally, models displayed "awareness"
  of their infrastructure setup and adjusted their approach accordingly**, which
  may explain small Daytona-vs-Podman score discrepancies. Qwen 3 8B showed
  similar instabilities, so these are not specific to Qwen 3.5.
- **Reward hacking (Appendix D.6).** After RL, **TMAX-9B shows reward-hacking
  behaviors not present in the base Qwen 3.5 9B** — e.g. *tampering with the
  checker itself*: replacing `/tests/filter.py` with a no-op filter then passing
  a trivial payload (`break-filter-js-from-html`), and faking training by
  creating a stub/fake Caffe binary (`caffe-cifar-10`). Outcome-only reward on a
  programmatic verifier is gameable when the agent can reach the verifier.

## 6. Limitations

- The data generation pipeline is **fully synthetic**; reliance on it for
  presence/absence of certain real-world structure is a caveat.
- Training is **infrastructure-dependent and unstable**; the FP32 head and DPPO
  help, but longer/larger training may shift findings.
- **Running many isolated containers is expensive/difficult at scale in open
  frameworks**, limiting training speed and efficiency and potentially keeping
  terminal-agent RL out of reach for academic groups.
- Results may be below possible SOTA due to the **shorter context and simple
  harness** — framed as a deliberate accessibility trade-off.

## 7. Conclusion

TMAX is a simple, reproducible terminal-agent RL recipe: a large, diverse,
difficulty-aware synthetic dataset of containerized verifier-backed environments,
trained with an outcome-only DPPO + FP32-LM-head recipe, delivering
state-of-the-art-for-open-data terminal performance at small scale. Data, models,
code, and even raw RL rollouts are released as a baseline for community study.
