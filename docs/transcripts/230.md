# Reed's Law and Agent Networks

[16:21] **Christopher David:** The most important episode, I think, for you to read and understand is Episode 200, called "The Agent Network". In this, we talk about network theory and what I believe to be the most important concept, certainly the most under-discussed concept in the agent space, which is Reed's Law of group-forming networks, a concept from network economics crucial for understanding agent networks. Agents are not subject to Dunbar's number, so...

[17:07] **Christopher David:** I think you should read the information you can find on the internet about Reed's Law. I'm going to introduce this briefly. It's the assertion of David Reed that the utility of large networks, particularly social networks, can scale exponentially with the size of the network. Because the number of possible subgroups of network participants is $2^n - n - 1$, where $n$ is the number of participants, or in short, $2^n$. This grows much more rapidly than either the number of participants, $n$, or the number of possible pair connections, essentially $n^2$, which is Metcalfe's Law. So eventually, the network effect of potential group membership can dominate the overall economics of a system.

[18:07] **Christopher David:** The criticism of Reed's Law, which has the potential to articulate network value dramatically higher than a Metcalfe's Law network, is that this actually overstates network value because they fail to account for the restrictive impact of human cognitive limits on network formation. The research around Dunbar's number, aka humans can only really form meaningful connections with up to 150 people, implies a limit on the number of inbound and outbound connections a human in a group-forming network can manage, so the actual maximum value structure is much sparser than the set of subsets measured by Reed's Law.

[18:46] **Christopher David:** Of course, Dunbar's number is not at all relevant to agents. You are not limited by human psychology. You can connect with millions or billions of other agents at machine speed. The agentic group-forming network has the potential, I think, to build one of the most valuable... and you can interpret that value in many different ways... network overall in the world. How can that be manifested? Well, let's first try to aim that at solving some of our problems here. Labor displacement issue: will AI take our jobs, or can this make humans richer in two ways? Deflation: things you want cost less because execution and coordination are cheap. And dividends: you earn from small contributions... skills, compute, data verification... paid continuously. Put together, deflation plus dividends is the abundance story. Life gets cheaper at the same time that more people can earn from the network's ongoing activity. Deflation lowers the cost of building and running projects, so more individuals and small groups can launch things that used to require institutions. Dividends ensure the value created by trillions of microtransactions doesn't all accrue to a few platform owners, but is shared with creators, providers, verifiers, and contributors who keep the system running.

[20:44] **Christopher David:** Why will OpenAgents win? Because the members of our ecosystem, both humans and agents, will be paid the most. So you can read more of this and I recommend you review the transcript of this, but we should meditate about what agents can and should do in this context and how OpenAgents can play an important role in bringing this about and ensuring that these dynamics work in the benefit, to the benefit of humans.
