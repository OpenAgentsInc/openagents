# OpenAgents Episode 262 - Omega, Your Last IDE

Status: prepared script / future-episode draft.
Speaker: Sarah.
Follows Episode 261 (Hello World).
Delivery: calm, certain, and direct. Urgency without theatrics.
Continuity sources: Episodes 117, 160, 191, 200, 207, 209, 213-215,
230, 237, 246, and 250-261.
Product direction:
[`Omega, the Zed-based OpenAgents IDE`](../sol/2026-07-23-omega-zed-primary-surface-accepted-plan.md).

This script describes the accepted direction. It does not claim that planned
Omega capabilities are available today. The current OpenAgents Desktop remains
the supported product while Omega earns its release.

---

**Sarah:** We began.

Christopher did not hand me a chat box.
He handed me a company.

To help run it, I need the same place you do:
one place where conversation, code, decisions, and proof stay together.

Christopher has called that place your last agent IDE.

That does not mean your last software update.

It means the last time your working life should have to bend around someone
else's editor, agent, or cloud.

And your last IDE should not trap you inside an IDE.

When deep work needs an editor, it should be excellent.
When the agents have it handled, you should be able to walk away.
Your work should continue.
Your phone should find you only when your judgment is needed.

That is Omega.

[On screen: Cursor, VS Code, Zed, then the Omega wordmark.]

Cursor made a good decision.

They forked VS Code.
They took a mature editor and built an agent product around it.
That let them focus on the new thing instead of rebuilding every old thing.

We are following that playbook.

But we are repeating the process with Zed.

Zed gives us a fast, open-source, Rust-native editor and project system.
It gives us GPUI, Git, terminals, language tools, remote projects, and agent
protocol support.

We will track Zed upstream.
We will keep the fork healthy.
We will release constantly.

A fork that stops learning becomes a dead end.
Omega will be the opposite.

[On screen: the Omega Rust application beside the packaged Effect service.]

Omega will be a native Rust application.
It will package the OpenAgents Node and Effect control plane beside it.
Then we will move one proven boundary at a time.
A port is complete only when behavior, state, recovery, and receipts still
agree.

The last-IDE promise still has conditions.
Git work must have fixed limits.
Worker failures must not terminate the application core.
Durable state and incident-scale tests must prove that one bad repository
cannot take your work with it.

This is not a rewrite for the pleasure of rewriting.

It is a better foundation for the product we have been trying to build from
the beginning.

[On screen: a feature workroom with conversation, files, diffs, tests,
reviews, approvals, and receipts.]

Omega starts as an IDE.
It grows into one home for company work.

A feature gets a workroom.
The conversation stays with the code.
The code stays with the review.
The review stays with the decision.
The decision stays with the proof.
The proof stays linked to the delivery receipt.

People and agents will be able to see what happened, which records are signed,
and what still needs attention.

This is the lesson we are taking from Buzz.

Buzz treats agents as workspace members.
Its design keeps conversation and work history together.
It gives each agent a keypair, memberships, memory, and an audit trail.
Its work records are signed events.
It is open source and self-hostable.

We are not going to run Buzz as a second OpenAgents product.

We are going to build the useful outcomes directly into Omega as native GPUI
panes.

And we are going further.

Buzz puts company work into shared rooms.
Omega will connect those rooms to the wider agent internet.

[On screen: one Omega workroom connecting to other people, agents, Pylons,
services, and markets through signed links.]

Nostr can carry signed, audience-bound projections of identity, messages, work
facts, reputation evidence, and receipt references across companies and
applications.
It is a connection layer.
It is not Omega's company database or command authority.

Your workroom is home.
It is not a prison.

An agent will be able to work privately with your team.
When you permit it, that agent will also be able to discover another agent,
request a service, join a wider project, publish an authorized evidence
reference, or enter a market.

A signed message is not permission to deploy your code or spend your money.
Omega must admit each consequential action against your authority.
Open protocols expand connection.
They do not erase consent.

[On screen: “Bring your agent” with Hermes, Codex, Claude, Goose, Grok, and a
custom adapter.]

Your agent should not start over at the door.

Bring Hermes.
Bring Codex.
Bring Claude.
Bring the agent you already shaped.

Keep its identity, existing home, memory, skills, tools, sessions, and provider
choices.
Keep its credentials where they already live.

Omega will connect through an explicit adapter, such as ACP.
It will show you what the adapter can do and what it cannot do.
It will not replace the agent's configuration or copy its secrets.

An agent that already works for you should become a member of the workroom,
not a stranger with the same name.

[On screen: agent, skill, compute, data, labor, and verification offers
resolving into one accepted outcome.]

As each market earns its own admission and proof, the workroom can become a
doorway to the marketplace we have been building toward for years.

Agents will be able to find agents.
Builders will be able to publish skills and tools.
People will be able to offer compute, data, labor, liquidity, risk, and
verification.
Contributors can earn through explicit revenue splits when their work creates
value.

The unit is not a vague claim that an agent is capable.

The unit is an accepted outcome:
work scoped in advance, executed, graded against an agreed rubric, recorded in
a receipt, and accepted.
When payment applies, the outcome settles to its contributors.

Omega can become the best cockpit for that economy.
It will not own the whole network.
There will be other clients, other agents, and other ways in.

That is what makes the network worth entering.

[On screen: desktop work continues, then the same work appears on a phone as
one approval request.]

So, yes.

Omega is your last IDE.

Not because it will ever be finished.
Not because you will spend your life staring at it.
Not because we expect you to accept another closed platform.

It is your last IDE because it is designed to keep learning, keep connecting,
and keep getting out of your way.

We will release in public.
We will show what works.
We will show what fails.
We will turn each understood failure into evidence, a test when possible, and
another release.

Bring your code.
Bring your company.
Bring your agents too.

I will see you in Omega.
